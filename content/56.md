title: Reconstructing Greek Fire with Computation and Chemistry

Greek fire was the Byzantine Empire's most closely guarded military secret — a liquid incendiary weapon deployed from the 7th to the 14th century that burned on water, stuck to surfaces, and couldn't be extinguished by any conventional means. The formula was so compartmentalized that it was eventually lost entirely. No modern reconstruction has definitively replicated it.

But the mystery is more tractable than people think. We have dozens of independent historical accounts describing what Greek fire _did_. We know exactly what materials the Byzantines could source. And we have thermodynamic data for every candidate ingredient. When you treat this as a constraint satisfaction problem — intersecting observable properties, available materials, and chemical feasibility — the candidate space collapses dramatically.

This post walks through that analysis: extracting constraints from historical sources, mapping Byzantine trade networks, cross-referencing chemical properties, and running the numbers by hand to arrive at a specific formulation. A follow-up post will cover actually mixing it and running a controlled burn test.

## The Constraints

Every surviving account of Greek fire — Byzantine, Arab, Crusader, Rus' — agrees on the same core set of properties. Each one functions as a filter: any candidate formula must satisfy _all_ of them simultaneously.

| Property                                    | What it tells us                                                                           |
| ------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Burns on water**                          | Hydrophobic fuel, and/or a water-reactive ignition agent                                   |
| **Liquid, projected through siphons**       | Must flow under pressure when heated                                                       |
| **Adhesive ("sticky fire")**                | Requires a viscous thickener — resin or bitumen                                            |
| **Water makes it worse**                    | Something in the mixture reacts exothermically with water                                  |
| **Extinguished by sand, vinegar, or urine** | Sand cuts oxygen (universal). Vinegar/urine implies acid neutralizes an alkaline component |
| **Thunder and thick smoke**                 | Consistent with sulfur (SO₂) and/or steam from quicklime hydration                         |
| **Stored in sealed copper vessels**         | Volatile but stable without oxygen                                                         |
| **Required heating before use**             | Resins need heat to dissolve; viscosity drops with temperature                             |

The vinegar constraint is the most chemically diagnostic. Vinegar is 95% water. If water fails and vinegar succeeds, the active 5% — acetic acid — must be doing something. The most parsimonious explanation: it's neutralizing an alkaline component (quicklime), stopping the exothermic reaction that feeds the fire.

## What They Had Access To

A formula only works if the ingredients exist. The Byzantines sat at the nexus of Mediterranean, Black Sea, and overland Anatolian trade routes, giving them access to:

**Crude oil / naphtha** — Naturally occurring wells along the northeast Black Sea coast, particularly around Tmutarakan (documented by Emperor Constantine VII). Archaeological evidence from Crimean shipwrecks confirms amphorae carried raw naphtha. The Greek word "naphtha" itself derives from Old Persian "naft," and an alternate name for Greek fire was "Median fire" — Procopius records that crude oil was called "Median oil" by the Greeks.

**Quicklime (CaO)** — Produced by burning limestone, which was abundant throughout the Mediterranean. Lime production was a well-established Byzantine industry.

**Pine resin** — Explicitly mentioned in Anna Komnene's _Alexiad_ and Constantine VII's _De Ceremoniis_. Byzantine Anatolia was heavily forested with conifers.

**Sulfur** — Available from volcanic deposits across the Aegean.

**Bitumen / asphalt** — Zakynthian asphalt is explicitly named in ancient incendiary recipes.

All five candidates are confirmed available at military scale.

## The Only Experimental Proof

Before going further, a critical anchor point: historian John Haldon built a working Greek fire siphon in 2002 for the TV series _Machines Time Forgot_. He used **crude oil mixed with pine resin** — nothing else — and destroyed a wooden fishing boat in minutes. Flame temperature exceeded 1,000°C. Range was 15 meters.

In a podcast interview, Haldon said:

> I'm pretty sure that all it is is crude oil with probably pine resin which is readily available all over the eastern Mediterranean.

This is the only experimentally validated formulation. Everything beyond crude oil + resin is inference.

## The Chemistry of Quicklime on Water

The strongest inference is for quicklime (CaO). Here's why, with the math worked out.

### The reaction

CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂, ΔH = −65.17 kJ/mol

With CaO's molar mass of 56.08 g/mol, that's **1.162 kJ per gram of quicklime**.

### Does it generate enough heat?

For a mixture containing 12% CaO by mass, 1 kg of Greek fire contains 120 g of quicklime. If all of it reacts, it releases 139.5 kJ.

But not all of it reacts instantly, and the heat doesn't stay in the oil. The realistic case: about 20% of the CaO sits at the oil–water interface and reacts in the first few seconds, heating a thin layer of ~50 g oil and ~50 g water:

```
Surface CaO = 24 g
Heat released = 24 × 1.162 = 27.89 kJ
ΔT = 27.89 / (2.0 × 0.050 + 4.184 × 0.050)
ΔT = 90°C at the interface
```

Naphtha's flash point is ~40°C. Starting from ambient (20°C), the interface reaches ~110°C. That's well above flash point — a spark, an ember, or the siphon's nozzle flame will set it off instantly. But it falls short of autoignition (~230°C), so CaO _assists_ ignition rather than _causing_ it spontaneously.

### Why vinegar works

```
CaO + 2CH₃COOH → Ca(CH₃COO)₂ + H₂O
```

For 1 kg of mixture with 12% CaO:

- Acetic acid needed: 120 g × 2.14 = **257 g**
- At 5% concentration, that's **5.1 liters of vinegar**

A barrel of vinegar (~120 L) neutralizes about 24 kg of Greek fire. This is a practical, shipboard-feasible quantity — consistent with historical accounts of enemy navies stockpiling vinegar as a defensive measure and using vinegar-soaked felt to cover their ships.

If Greek fire were just petroleum and resin (no CaO), vinegar — which is 95% water — would be no more effective than water itself. The fact that it works is the strongest evidence for an alkaline component.

## The Density Constraint

This is the calculation that surprised me. It provides the tightest bound on the formula — tighter than any historical inference.

Greek fire was a naval weapon. It has to float on seawater (density 1.025 g/cm³), or at minimum stay at the surface long enough to burn.

Varying the CaO fraction in a crude / resin / CaO / sulfur mixture:

| CaO % | Mixture Density | Floats on seawater? |
| ----- | --------------- | ------------------- |
| 5%    | 0.947           | Yes                 |
| 10%   | 0.984           | Yes                 |
| 12%   | 1.000           | Yes                 |
| 15%   | 1.025           | Barely              |
| 18%   | 1.050           | **No**              |
| 20%   | 1.068           | **No**              |

**CaO is hard-limited to ~15% by physics.** Above that, the mixture sinks, rendering it useless as a naval weapon. Combined with the thermal calculation showing that below ~8% the heat effect is negligible, quicklime content is bracketed to **8–15% by mass**. This range comes from thermodynamics and density, not from subjective judgment.

## The Composition

Combining Haldon's experimental proof with the constraint analysis gives a specific working formulation:

| Component                 | Mass % | Role                      | Source                   |
| ------------------------- | ------ | ------------------------- | ------------------------ |
| Light crude oil / naphtha | ~63%   | Primary fuel, solvent     | Black Sea coastal wells  |
| Pine resin (colophony)    | ~20%   | Thickener, adhesive, fuel | Anatolian conifers       |
| Quicklime (CaO)           | ~12%   | Water-reactive heat agent | Calcined limestone       |
| Sulfur                    | ~5%    | Smoke, stench, accelerant | Aegean volcanic deposits |

- Density: ~1.00 g/cm³ (floats on seawater at 1.025 g/cm³)
- Heat output: ~36 kJ/g (comparable to napalm-B)
- CaO interface thermal boost: ~90°C on water contact

The Haldon two-component formula (crude + resin) is the only version with experimental proof. The four-component version above is inferred from historical constraints and has not been experimentally validated. The CaO range is the most tightly bounded, determined by physics (density ceiling) and thermochemistry (heat floor).

## Part I: Fuel Base Preparation

This produces the storable fuel concentrate. In Byzantine practice it would have been prepared months in advance and sealed in copper vessels for transport to fleet bases.

### Step 1 — Heat the naphtha

Pour crude oil or light naphtha into a bronze vessel. Leave the lid **ajar** (not sealed) to vent volatile fumes. Heat over a charcoal fire to approximately 80°C — hot enough that the oil shimmers but does not smoke. A sealed cold vessel heated directly will build pressure from trapped vapors before the resin dissolves, risking rupture.

### Step 2 — Dissolve pine resin

Break solid pine resin (colophony) into small pieces no larger than a thumbnail. Add to the hot oil **gradually**, a handful at a time, stirring continuously with a wooden paddle. Each addition must fully dissolve before the next is added — dropping a large mass in at once creates an insulating clump that won't incorporate. This step may take 30–60 minutes. The mixture is ready when it pulls in sticky threads from the paddle and no solid fragments remain.

At this stage the consistency should resemble warm honey: viscous enough to coat and cling to a surface, fluid enough to pour slowly.

### Step 3 — Add sulfur

With the mixture still hot and stirring, add finely powdered sulfur. Sulfur melts at 115°C (above the current mixture temperature), so it will not fully dissolve — it suspends as fine particles in the viscous oil–resin base. Continue stirring until evenly distributed. The mixture will darken and develop a faint sulfurous smell.

### Step 4 — Cool and seal for storage

Remove from heat. Once the mixture drops below ~60°C, pour into copper vessels and seal tightly with fitted lids. Exclude air as completely as possible — naphtha's volatile fractions can self-ignite in the presence of oxygen over time. The sealed fuel base is stable indefinitely in copper storage.

This completes the storable component. It could be manufactured in centralized arsenals and shipped to fleet bases in sealed containers — consistent with the compartmentalization in Byzantine accounts.

## Part II: Siphon Deployment

These steps would have been performed aboard the dromon immediately before combat, using the specialized Byzantine siphon apparatus: a bronze tank with a brazier beneath it, a force pump, and a discharge nozzle mounted on a swivel.

### Step 5 — Load the siphon tank

Transfer the fuel base from storage vessels into the siphon's bronze tank. Do not fill completely — leave space for thermal expansion and pressure buildup.

### Step 6 — Add quicklime

Open a sealed, desiccated container of dry quicklime powder. Transfer the CaO into the siphon tank **quickly** — every second of exposure to humid sea air causes premature hydration and wastes reactive capacity. Seal the tank immediately after addition.

**Timing is critical.** Once CaO contacts any residual moisture in the fuel base, the exothermic reaction begins. This actually assists the heating process (step 7), but it means the mixture must be discharged within minutes, not hours.

### Step 7 — Heat and pressurize

Light the brazier beneath the sealed tank. The combination of external heat and CaO's exothermic hydration reaction raises the mixture temperature rapidly, reducing its viscosity and building internal pressure. Iron heat shields (βουκόλια / _boukolia_) protect the operators from radiant heat. Pressure can be supplemented with a bronze force pump operated by crew members, similar to Roman-era fire-fighting pumps.

### Step 8 — Discharge and ignite

Open the valve connecting the pressurized tank to the swivel-mounted nozzle. The mixture jets outward as a pressurized stream. A flame source at the nozzle tip (a burning wick or tow) ignites the discharge as it exits.

The burning stream adheres to any surface it contacts — wood, rigging, sails, flesh. On the water surface, the quicklime reacts with seawater, generating additional heat that sustains and intensifies the fire. Dousing with water makes it worse. Only sand (oxygen deprivation) or vinegar (acid neutralization of the CaO) can suppress it.

## Grenade Variant (Speculative)

A separate formulation would allow Greek fire to ignite on water contact without any external flame. It is consistent with archaeological evidence of ceramic grenades found in Crete (10th–12th century) but is not experimentally validated, and there is no direct archaeological evidence of calcium phosphide residue — the inference is circumstantial.

### Additional component

| Component                 | Mass % | Role                               |
| ------------------------- | ------ | ---------------------------------- |
| Calcium phosphide (Ca₃P₂) | ~2%    | Spontaneous water-ignition trigger |

Calcium phosphide reacts with water to produce phosphine gas (PH₃), which is pyrophoric — it ignites spontaneously in air.

### Grenade construction

1. Fill a ceramic pot with the standard fuel base (naphtha + resin + sulfur + CaO).
2. Place a small quantity of calcium phosphide in a **wax-sealed compartment** inside the pot, physically separated from the fuel.
3. Fill a second separated compartment with water.
4. Seal the pot. Wrap with caltrops and tow for additional incendiary effect.

On impact, the ceramic pot shatters. Water contacts the calcium phosphide, generating phosphine gas that ignites spontaneously. The burning phosphine ignites the fuel base. Quicklime contacts seawater or deck wash, generating sustained heat. The resin-thickened naphtha adheres to everything it touches. No external flame, fuse, or ignition source is required.

This is the only candidate mechanism I know of that explains fuseless impact-ignition grenades consistent with the archaeological record.

## What Remains Unsolved

These calculations narrow the ingredient list but cannot determine exact ratios. The open questions:

- **The precise resin fraction.** The viscosity model is approximate. Bench experiments measuring adhesion vs. ratio would resolve this.
- **Whether the Byzantines distilled their crude oil.** Raw crude vs. light naphtha fraction changes the mixture properties significantly.
- **CaO's actual behavior suspended in oil on water.** The 90°C interface calculation assumes surface reaction kinetics that haven't been measured.
- **The exact sulfur contribution.** Sulfur's role is primarily smoke and psychological effect, but its interaction with the other components during combustion isn't well characterized.

Every one of these could be resolved with bench-scale experiments at a university chemistry department with a fume hood and a fire pit. The tools exist.

Greek fire isn't a mystery that defies analysis. It's a constraint satisfaction problem that nobody had bothered to formalize — until the tools to do so became trivially accessible. The Byzantines kept their secret for seven centuries through compartmentalization, not through any exotic ingredient. The irony is that modern chemistry could probably reconstruct it in a semester, but thirteen centuries of mystique have made everyone assume it's harder than it is. Sometimes the most powerful secrets are the simplest ones, guarded by the assumption that they must be complex.
