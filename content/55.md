---
title: The Constitution's Self-Destruct Clause
date: 2026-04-12
---

Article V of the US Constitution describes how to amend it. It has only two substantive restrictions on what an amendment can say: before 1808, you couldn't touch the slave trade or direct tax clauses, and you can never strip a state's equal vote in the Senate without that state's consent.

Everything else is purely procedural. If two-thirds of both chambers of Congress propose an amendment (or two-thirds of state legislatures call a convention), and three-fourths of the states ratify it, the amendment becomes part of the Constitution. There is no rule saying "an amendment cannot abolish democracy" or "an amendment cannot contradict the Bill of Rights."

This means an amendment could legally concentrate all government power in one person, eliminate elections, abolish the courts, or repeal every other part of the Constitution — including Article V itself, making the change permanent. The only thing standing in the way is the political difficulty of getting supermajorities to vote for it.

Kurt Gödel reportedly noticed this before his 1947 citizenship exam and was so alarmed that Einstein and Morgenstern, who accompanied him as character witnesses, tried to dissuade him — but he raised it anyway, and the judge quickly moved on. He never published his argument, but the [Lean proof](data/GoedelLoophole.lean) formalizes it: when you encode Article V's validity conditions as a logical predicate, any amendment that avoids the two narrow provisos passes with the predicate reducing to $\top \land \top$. The Constitution contains the legal machinery for its own total replacement.
